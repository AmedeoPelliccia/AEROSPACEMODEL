## Feasibility Assessment of a Reusable SSTO Spaceplane

### 1. Context

Low-cost, routine access to space remains a persistent objective in aerospace engineering. Among reusable launch concepts, a **Single-Stage-to-Orbit (SSTO) spaceplane** is one of the most demanding and potentially transformative architectures. Such a vehicle would integrate atmospheric take-off, acceleration through multiple flight regimes, orbital insertion without staging, controlled de-orbit, atmospheric re-entry, and horizontal runway recovery within a single reusable airframe.

This assessment addresses the **orbital SSTO mission only**. It does **not** treat hypersonic point-to-point transport as an equivalent mission class, although some enabling technologies may overlap.

### 2. Assessment Objective

The objective is to determine whether a **reusable runway-operable SSTO spaceplane** is feasible using near-term or foreseeable technology, and to identify the governing constraints, uncertainties, and pacing technologies that control viability.

The assessment shall determine whether such a vehicle can be made:

- **physically plausible**
- **technically defensible**
- **operationally supportable**
- **economically meaningful**
- **regulatorily certifiable or licensable within a credible framework**

### 3. System Under Assessment

The candidate concept is a **reusable integrated airframe-propulsion orbital vehicle** characterised by:

- single-stage-to-orbit mission profile
- horizontal take-off and horizontal landing
- runway-based operations from dedicated or adapted spaceport infrastructure
- ascent through subsonic, transonic, supersonic, hypersonic, and exo-atmospheric flight regimes
- fully reusable primary structure and thermal protection system
- cryogenic propellant architecture (hydrogen and oxygen)
- integrated propulsion-airframe design

### 4. Core Problem Definition

The central engineering question is:

> Can a reusable SSTO spaceplane achieve orbital insertion and runway recovery in a single stage while preserving sufficient payload, acceptable operability, credible safety, and reusable system integrity?

This is governed by seven deeply coupled sub-problems:

1. Propulsion closure across the full Mach-altitude envelope
2. Dry-mass fraction closure
3. Aerothermal survivability and reusability
4. Stability, guidance, and control through all flight phases
5. Trajectory closure under structural, thermal, and propulsive constraints
6. Operational turnaround and maintenance burden
7. Regulatory compatibility across aviation and launch domains

### 5. Scope of Assessment

The study shall generate evidence in nine areas:

1. Mission and Architecture Definition
2. Propulsion Feasibility
3. Mass Fraction and Structural Closure
4. Thermal Protection and Heat Management
5. Trajectory Analysis
6. Stability and Control Assessment
7. Operability and Turnaround
8. Regulatory and Certification Pathway
9. Economic Screening

### 6. Reference Assessment Targets

- **Payload:** 5–10 metric tons to ~200 km circular LEO
- **Reusability:** Full vehicle reuse without primary hardware discard
- **Turnaround aspiration:** ≤ 48 hours (subject to technical validation)
- **Cost stress-test target:** < $10M per mission
- **Safety:** Decomposed by loss-of-mission, loss-of-vehicle, loss-of-crew, and public-risk

### 7. Primary Uncertainties

1. Propulsion mode transition
2. Mass fraction closure
3. Reusable TPS closure
4. Cryogenic structural integration
5. Abort and safe-return coverage
6. Regulatory basis
7. Economic competitiveness

### 8. Assessment Logic

A positive outcome requires credible closure in **all** dimensions: propulsion, mass fraction, thermal survivability, GNC, trajectory, reusability/operability, regulatory pathway, and comparative economics. Failure to close any one dimension may invalidate the concept.

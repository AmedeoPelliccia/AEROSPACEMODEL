## Feasibility Assessment of a Reusable SSTO Spaceplane

### 1. Context

Low-cost, routine access to space remains a persistent objective in aerospace engineering. Among reusable launch concepts, a **Single-Stage-to-Orbit (SSTO) spaceplane** is one of the most demanding and potentially transformative architectures. Such a vehicle would integrate atmospheric take-off, acceleration through multiple flight regimes, orbital insertion without staging, controlled de-orbit, atmospheric re-entry, and horizontal runway recovery within a single reusable airframe.

This assessment addresses the **orbital SSTO mission only**. It does **not** treat hypersonic point-to-point transport as an equivalent mission class, although some enabling technologies may overlap.

### 2. Assessment Objective

The objective is to determine whether a **reusable runway-operable SSTO spaceplane** is feasible using near-term or foreseeable technology, and to identify the governing constraints, uncertainties, and pacing technologies that control viability.

The assessment shall determine whether such a vehicle can be made:

- **physically plausible**
- **technically defensible**
- **operationally supportable**
- **economically meaningful**
- **regulatorily certifiable or licensable within a credible framework**

### 2A. Mission Needs

**Mission needs** define the operational gaps and strategic drivers that justify assessment of a reusable SSTO spaceplane. They represent the **why** of the programme and do not constitute fixed system requirements. Instead, they establish the capability rationale that subsequent requirements, KNOTs, KNUs, and assessment criteria must validate, refine, or reject.

#### 2A.1 Primary Mission Need

The fundamental driver is the **need** for a launch system that combines:

- Direct runway-based operations
- Full vehicle recovery without staging
- High-tempo reusability
- Reduced mission complexity
- Rapid return-to-service

The purpose is not merely to reach orbit, but to determine whether orbital access can be delivered by a vehicle whose operations, recovery logic, and support burden more closely resemble those of a reusable aerospace platform than those of a disposable launch stack.

#### 2A.2 Capability Need

The system is intended to address the capability gap between:

- **Multi-stage launch vehicles:** systems that achieve orbit efficiently but rely on staging and specialised launch infrastructure.
- **Atmospheric hypersonic vehicles:** systems that offer runway operability but do not achieve orbital insertion and recovery within one reusable vehicle.

The mission need is to determine whether a single integrated vehicle can execute the full mission profile:

1. Runway departure
2. Ascent through atmospheric and hypersonic regimes
3. Orbital insertion without stage separation
4. Controlled de-orbit and re-entry
5. Horizontal runway landing
6. Repeatable reuse with manageable refurbishment burden

#### 2A.3 Operational Need

Current orbital access systems impose substantial operational burdens, including stage integration logistics, launch-pad dependence, limited cadence, and extensive refurbishment. The SSTO concept is driven by the need for:

- Simplified sortie logic
- Reduced mission interfaces
- Higher dispatch potential
- Compressed turnaround cycles
- Integrated flight and recovery operations

#### 2A.4 Reusability Need

A central mission need is to determine whether **true full-vehicle reuse** can be operationally viable. This requires determining whether:

- the airframe can survive repeated ascent and re-entry cycles,
- the Thermal Protection System (TPS) can support reuse without prohibitive refurbishment,
- propulsion and cryogenic systems can tolerate repeated thermal and mechanical loads,
- landing, inspection, and reflight can be performed within a realistic operational model.

#### 2A.5 Infrastructure Need

The concept addresses the need for a launch architecture that reduces exclusive dependence on specialised vertical-launch infrastructure. The mission must determine whether orbital operations can be supported by:

- runway-based spaceport infrastructure,
- adapted terrestrial flight operations systems,
- integrated ground servicing for cryogenic loading, inspection, and turnaround.

This implies an **aircraft-like infrastructure model**, not the use of unmodified civil airports.

#### 2A.6 Economic Need

The mission must determine whether an SSTO architecture can offer a defensible economic advantage relative to existing or emerging systems. The objective is not to assume cost superiority, but to determine whether the concept can plausibly support:

- lower recurring mission costs,
- improved asset utilisation,
- reduced hardware attrition,
- higher flight frequency,
- reduced operational friction.

Assessment shall be comparative against **partially reusable multi-stage systems**, not expendable launchers alone.

#### 2A.7 Safety and Recovery Need

The mission requires recovery behaviour that is controlled, repeatable, and operationally integrated, rather than dependent on expendable or non-recoverable mission logic. This includes the need for:

- controlled return from orbit,
- guided atmospheric re-entry,
- runway landing under defined recovery conditions,
- credible safe-return logic for off-nominal scenarios,
- inspection discipline compatible with sustained reuse.

#### 2A.8 Regulatory Need

Current governance structures separate aerospace activity into aviation airworthiness and launch/re-entry licensing domains. The mission need therefore includes determining whether a unified or hybrid approval basis is feasible for a vehicle that:

- departs as an aircraft-like system,
- ascends as a launch vehicle,
- re-enters from space,
- recovers as a runway-landing aerospace vehicle.

#### 2A.9 Technology Maturation Need

The mission functions as a **forcing case** for the technologies that determine concept credibility. These include:

- combined-cycle or hybrid propulsion integration,
- reusable high-temperature structures and TPS,
- integrated cryogenic structural tanks,
- active thermal management,
- control authority across extreme Mach-altitude transitions.

#### 2A.10 Strategic Need

At the strategic level, the mission may support objectives such as:

- autonomous orbital access,
- responsive payload deployment,
- growth in dual-use aerospace capability,
- development of reusable high-speed aerospace infrastructure.

These strategic motives may justify the assessment effort, but they remain subordinate to technical, operational, and economic evidence.

### 2B. Mission Need Statements

The mission needs are formalised as follows.

**MN-01 — Direct Reusable Orbital Access**
The programme needs to determine whether orbit can be reached and recovered by a **single reusable vehicle without stage discard**.

**MN-02 — Runway-Based Operability**
The programme needs to determine whether orbital missions can be executed through **horizontal take-off and landing** from runway-based spaceport infrastructure.

**MN-03 — Full-Envelope Vehicle Integration**
The programme needs a vehicle concept capable of traversing **subsonic, supersonic, hypersonic, exo-atmospheric, and re-entry regimes** within one coherent system.

**MN-04 — Operationally Meaningful Reuse**
The programme needs reuse to extend beyond nominal recovery and support **inspection, servicing, and return-to-flight** within a realistic operational model.

**MN-05 — Reduced Mission Complexity**
The programme needs to assess whether elimination of staging reduces mission interfaces, recovery complexity, and hardware attrition sufficiently to justify the architecture.

**MN-06 — Controlled Return and Recovery**
The programme needs a concept capable of **controlled de-orbit, guided re-entry, and runway recovery** rather than expendable or non-recoverable mission logic.

**MN-07 — Viable Infrastructure Model**
The programme needs to determine whether the concept can operate from a ground architecture involving **runway operations, cryogenic servicing, inspection, and turnaround support**.

**MN-08 — Economic Defensibility**
The programme needs to determine whether a reusable SSTO system could achieve sufficient **operational or recurring-cost advantage** to remain competitive against reusable multi-stage alternatives.

**MN-09 — Certifiable Governance Basis**
The programme needs to determine whether the vehicle can be governed under a coherent framework spanning **airworthiness, launch, re-entry, and public-risk control**.

**MN-10 — Pacing-Technology Identification**
The programme needs to identify which technologies and uncertainties dominate design closure, and whether those can mature within a credible planning horizon.

### 2C. Mission Need Boundaries

To maintain scope clarity, the following boundaries apply:

- The mission need addresses **orbital SSTO feasibility**, not generic hypersonic transport.
- The mission need does **not** assume that all stated aspirations are simultaneously achievable.
- The mission need does **not** establish fixed payload, cost, turnaround, safety, certification, or licensing requirements.
- The mission need exists to support later derivation of:
  - System Requirements
  - Key Performance Parameters (KPPs)
  - Measures of Effectiveness (MOEs)
  - KNOTs
  - KNUs
  - Assessment Criteria
  - Programme go / no-go decision criteria

### 2D. Mission Need Summary

The mission need is to determine whether a **fully reusable, runway-operable, single-stage orbital vehicle** can serve as a technically credible and operationally meaningful alternative to staged systems by combining:

- direct runway-based sortie logic,
- single-vehicle ascent to orbit,
- controlled return and landing,
- repeatable reuse,
- manageable operational burden,
- credible regulatory and economic closure.

### 3. System Under Assessment

The candidate concept is a **reusable integrated airframe-propulsion orbital vehicle** characterised by:

- single-stage-to-orbit mission profile
- horizontal take-off and horizontal landing
- runway-based operations from dedicated or adapted spaceport infrastructure
- ascent through subsonic, transonic, supersonic, hypersonic, and exo-atmospheric flight regimes
- fully reusable primary structure and thermal protection system
- cryogenic propellant architecture (hydrogen and oxygen)
- integrated propulsion-airframe design

### 4. Core Problem Definition

The central engineering question is:

> Can a reusable SSTO spaceplane achieve orbital insertion and runway recovery in a single stage while preserving sufficient payload, acceptable operability, credible safety, and reusable system integrity?

This is governed by seven deeply coupled sub-problems:

1. Propulsion closure across the full Mach-altitude envelope
2. Dry-mass fraction closure
3. Aerothermal survivability and reusability
4. Stability, guidance, and control through all flight phases
5. Trajectory closure under structural, thermal, and propulsive constraints
6. Operational turnaround and maintenance burden
7. Regulatory compatibility across aviation and launch domains

### 5. Scope of Assessment

The study shall generate evidence in nine areas:

1. Mission and Architecture Definition
2. Propulsion Feasibility
3. Mass Fraction and Structural Closure
4. Thermal Protection and Heat Management
5. Trajectory Analysis
6. Stability and Control Assessment
7. Operability and Turnaround
8. Regulatory and Certification Pathway
9. Economic Screening

### 6. Reference Assessment Targets

- **Payload:** 5–10 metric tons to ~200 km circular LEO
- **Reusability:** Full vehicle reuse without primary hardware discard
- **Turnaround aspiration:** ≤ 48 hours (subject to technical validation)
- **Cost stress-test target:** < USD 10M per mission
- **Safety:** Decomposed by loss-of-mission, loss-of-vehicle, loss-of-crew, and public-risk

### 7. Primary Uncertainties

1. Propulsion mode transition
2. Mass fraction closure
3. Reusable TPS closure
4. Cryogenic structural integration
5. Abort and safe-return coverage
6. Regulatory basis
7. Economic competitiveness

### 8. Assessment Logic

A positive outcome requires credible closure in **all** dimensions: propulsion, mass fraction, thermal survivability, GNC, trajectory, reusability/operability, regulatory pathway, and comparative economics. Failure to close any one dimension may invalidate the concept.
